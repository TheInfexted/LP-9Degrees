# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Nightclub owners and operators in Kuala Lumpur, Malaysia (and the wider region) who are looking for a marketing partner that already understands their world. They're not browsing for ideas at 10am from a desk; they're checking the page late, often on a phone, after seeing the agency tagged on a club's Instagram or referenced by another operator. They want fast proof that 9Degrees can fill a room, and a frictionless way to start a conversation, not a contact form they'll abandon.

A secondary audience is talent and venue partners (DJs, brands, event organisers) who land here from a tagged story and need to know within 10 seconds that 9Degrees is the agency behind 404 and Nono.

The job to be done: confirm "these are the people who run KL's nights" and start a WhatsApp or Instagram chat without thinking about it.

## Product Purpose

A single landing page for 9Degrees, a Kuala Lumpur nightlife marketing and advertising agency. The page exists to convert curious operators and partners into direct conversations on WhatsApp or Instagram. It is not a portfolio site, not a blog, not a service catalogue. It's a runway.

Success looks like: a visitor lands from a club's Instagram tag, scrolls once, recognises the venues 9Degrees has worked on (404, Nono), feels the vibe is right, taps WhatsApp, and is in a chat within 30 seconds.

The agency's reputation is built inside the rooms it markets, not on a website. The site's job is to translate that reputation into a credible online surface that doesn't undercut it.

## Brand Personality

Loud, on-brand with the venues, kinetic. Confident in the way someone who actually runs a packed room is confident: not loud about being loud, just unmistakably the people who pulled this off. Three words: kinetic, after-hours, certain.

Voice is short, declarative, slightly cocky in the way a club's own social copy is. Headlines should read like a poster, not a pitch deck. Body copy is sparing: every line earns its space because most visitors will not read more than three.

Emotional goals: visitors should feel the page is current (this week, not last year), that it was made by people who go to these clubs, and that messaging the agency is the obvious next move.

## Anti-references

The page must not look or feel like any of these:

- **Generic nightclub website.** No fog particles, no confetti animations, no neon pink-on-cyan gradients smeared across the hero, no stock photography of crowds with hands in the air. The cliche pile.
- **Corporate agency boilerplate.** No "we tell stories" hero copy. No identical-card service grids ("Strategy / Creative / Production"). No stock photos of a team laughing at a laptop. No 6-step process diagram.
- **Crypto / Web3 aesthetic.** No animated 3D blob rendered behind the hero. No metallic chrome typography. No cyber-glitch font for "404" just because the club's name is 404.
- **Generic SaaS / tech startup.** No cream off-white background. No oversized rounded "Get Started" button. No gradient text headers. No three-up feature cards.

The trap to dodge: visitors should not be able to guess the aesthetic from the category. "Nightlife agency website" must not produce a black background with cyan-to-magenta gradients, a hero video of a club crowd, and a "Book a Discovery Call" button. If the design feels predictable from the brief, restart.

## Design Principles

1. **Loud, but never generic.** Energy and attitude executed with editorial discipline. The page should feel kinetic and on-brand with the clubs, but always two steps off the obvious nightclub-website template. Reach for the Boiler Room / Resident Advisor / Mixmag editorial register before reaching for festival-promo bombast.

2. **Made by the people who make the night.** Every visual and copy decision should feel like it came from someone who actually runs 404 and Nono, not someone who searched "nightclub website" for moodboards. Operator authenticity beats agency polish. If a choice would feel out of place on the venues' own Instagram grids, it's wrong here.

3. **Show the rooms, not the pitch.** The venues, posters, and nights are the proof. Lead with the work, the names, and the visual evidence of full rooms. Skip generic agency framing (service ladders, "our process", "we believe..."). The page argues by example, not by claim.

4. **WhatsApp is the only conversion.** The entire page is a runway to a chat on WhatsApp or Instagram. There are no forms, no email capture, no lead funnels. Every section ends near a way to message. A visitor who never reads more than the hero should still know how to start a conversation in one tap.

5. **Cinema, not the festival lineup.** Heavy photo crops, deep blacks, decisive typography, set-list rhythms over party-promo decoration. Restraint is the source of authority. The page should feel like the visual language of a club night documented well, not a poster trying to sell tickets.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.2 AA target. English only.

- Color contrast: minimum 4.5:1 for body text and 3:1 for large text and UI controls, even on dark / image backgrounds. Where photos sit behind text, an overlay or solid panel must guarantee the ratio.
- Motion: meaningful animations are part of the brand, but every motion effect must respect `prefers-reduced-motion` and have a non-animated fallback. Auto-playing video is muted by default with controls available.
- Keyboard: full keyboard navigation, visible focus rings (designed, not the browser default), no focus traps in any modal or carousel.
- Touch targets: minimum 44x44px for all tap-to-message buttons. The CTAs to WhatsApp and Instagram must be the most reliable interactive elements on the page on mobile.
- Screen readers: meaningful alt text on venue and event photography (event name, venue, date when relevant). Decorative images marked aria-hidden. Headings form a single logical outline.
